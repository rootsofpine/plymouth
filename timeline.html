Markers of Time in the Pemigewasset Valley

A Chronicle of Plymouth

From its earliest days at the confluence of rivers to its growth as a center of education and community, Plymouth's story is written in the land itself. This timeline traces the significant moments that have shaped our town's character and spirit.

Foundations & Frontiers

The Colonial Era

1712

Conflict in the Pemigewasset Valley

During Queen Anne's War, Captain Thomas Baker burned an Abenaki village at the site of present-day Plymouth[citation:1]. This event reflects the complex colonial history that preceded the town's official founding, a reminder of the land's deeper history before settlement.

The land that would become Plymouth had been home to indigenous communities long before colonial settlement.
1763

Incorporation of New Plymouth

Plymouth was officially incorporated, named "New Plymouth" after the original Plymouth Colony in Massachusetts[citation:1]. Colonial Governor Benning Wentworth granted the town to soldiers from Hollis who had served in the French and Indian War, some of whom traced their origins to Plymouth, Massachusetts[citation:1].

1806

A Young Lawyer's First Case

A young attorney named Daniel Webster lost his first criminal case at the Plymouth courthouse[citation:1]. This building would later become home to the Plymouth Historical Society, connecting legal history with community preservation.

Growth & Transformation

The 19th Century

1845 & 1860

Expanding Borders

Plymouth expanded its territory by annexing parts of the neighboring towns of Hebron and Campton[citation:1][citation:5]. This period of growth reflected the town's increasing importance as a regional center in the Pemigewasset Valley.

1864

Literary History in Plymouth

The author Nathaniel Hawthorne died in Plymouth while on vacation with former U.S. President Franklin Pierce[citation:1]. He was staying at the Pemigewasset House, a prominent hotel that would later be destroyed by fire in 1909[citation:1].

1871

A Foundation for Education

The Plymouth Normal School was founded, emerging from the already established Holmes Plymouth Academy[citation:1]. This institution represented New Hampshire's commitment to teacher education and would become the seed from which Plymouth State University grew.

Modern Identity

20th Century & Beyond

Early 1900s

Plymouth's Connection to Baseball Greats

The Draper and Maynard Sporting Goods Company (D&M), based in Plymouth, supplied equipment directly to the Boston Red Sox[citation:1]. Legendary players including Babe Ruth would visit Plymouth to select their gear[citation:1][citation:7], creating a unique connection between this small New Hampshire town and America's pastime.

1939

Plymouth Teachers College

The institution evolved from the Normal School to become Plymouth Teachers College[citation:1]. This change reflected a broader educational mission while maintaining its core focus on preparing educators for New Hampshire's schools.

1963

Becoming Plymouth State College

Marking two centuries since the town's incorporation, the educational institution was renamed Plymouth State College[citation:1]. This change signaled an expansion of academic programs beyond teacher education.

2003

Plymouth State University

The institution achieved university status, becoming Plymouth State University[citation:1]. With over 3,300 undergraduate and 500 graduate students as of 2023/2024[citation:1], it solidified its role as an economic, medical, commercial, and cultural center for the region[citation:1].

2020s

Plymouth Today

Plymouth continues as the economic and cultural hub for the predominantly rural Plymouth Labor Market Area[citation:1]. With a population of 6,682 as of the 2020 census[citation:1], it maintains its unique role at the foot of the White Mountains, bridging the Lakes Region and the White Mountains as its motto declares.
