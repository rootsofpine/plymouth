Symbol of Sovereignty

The "Appeal to Heaven" Pine Tree Flag

The Pine Tree Flag: Symbol of New England's Revolutionary Spirit

A Flag Born of Resistance

The Pine Tree Flag, also known as the "Appeal to Heaven" flag, first flew during the American Revolutionary War as a symbol of New England's resistance to British rule. The white pine tree represented the region's valuable timber resources—particularly the towering Eastern White Pine reserved for the Royal Navy's masts.

"We have no other alternative than independence, or the most ignominious and galling servitude. The legions of our enemies thicken on our plains; desolation and death mark their bloody career."

— John Adams, 1775

The phrase "An Appeal to Heaven" comes from John Locke's Second Treatise on Government, which argued that when all other remedies have been exhausted, people have the right to appeal to heaven for justice. This philosophical foundation resonated deeply with the people of New England, who saw their struggle as both a practical and spiritual fight for self-determination.

For Plymouth, this flag symbolizes more than historical rebellion—it represents the enduring spirit of independence that has defined our community since its founding. The white pine, our state tree, stands as a living testament to this legacy.

Philosophical Roots

Transcendentalism & New England

The Soul in the Wilderness

Transcendentalism emerged in the early 19th century as a philosophical and literary movement centered in Concord, Massachusetts—just a day's journey from Plymouth. Led by thinkers like Ralph Waldo Emerson, Henry David Thoreau, and Margaret Fuller, it emphasized:

  • The inherent goodness of people and nature
  • Individual intuition over established doctrine
  • Self-reliance and independence of thought
  • Nature as a pathway to spiritual enlightenment
  • Civil disobedience against unjust laws

Thoreau's time at Walden Pond and his advocacy for simple living in harmony with nature directly parallel the practical self-sufficiency that characterized early Plymouth settlers. His essay "Civil Disobedience" echoes the same revolutionary spirit that inspired the Pine Tree Flag decades earlier.

"I went to the woods because I wished to live deliberately, to front only the essential facts of life, and see if I could not learn what it had to teach, and not, when I came to die, discover that I had not lived."

— Henry David Thoreau, Walden

Our Local Legacy

Plymouth's Dual Heritage

Practical Independence

From its founding in 1763, Plymouth was settled by people embodying practical self-reliance. The town's location at the confluence of the Pemigewasset and Baker rivers required resilience, resourcefulness, and a deep understanding of the natural world—qualities that aligned perfectly with transcendentalist values.

The White Pine as Symbol

The Eastern White Pine wasn't just timber—it was a symbol of vertical aspiration. These towering trees, some reaching 150 feet, represented the connection between earth and sky, the practical and the spiritual. For Plymouth's settlers and transcendentalists alike, the pine embodied growth, resilience, and upward striving.

Rivers of Reflection

The Pemigewasset and Baker rivers served as both practical transportation routes and sources of spiritual reflection. Like Thoreau's Walden Pond, these waterways provided space for contemplation, connection with nature, and the development of an independent spirit that defines Plymouth to this day.
