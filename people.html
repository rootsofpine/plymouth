Individual Souls Shaping a Collective Spirit

Faces of the Forest

From early settlers to modern stewards, Plymouth has been shaped by remarkable individuals whose lives embody the independent, transcendentalist spirit of this land. Their stories form the living roots of our community, each contributing to the enduring character of our town.

Notable Plymouth Figures

Early Settlers & Founders

1712

Captain Thomas Baker

Military Captain & Early Figure

During Queen Anne's War, Baker led an expedition that burned an Abenaki village at the site of present-day Plymouth. This controversial figure represents the complex early history of the Pemigewasset Valley, where colonial ambitions met indigenous communities.

"His actions during Queen Anne's War marked a violent turning point in the valley's history."
Plymouth's earliest colonial history
1806

Daniel Webster

Statesman & Orator

As a young lawyer, Webster tried his first criminal case in Plymouth—and lost. This early setback for the future statesman and orator marks Plymouth's place in New Hampshire's legal history. The courthouse where he argued would later become home to the Plymouth Historical Society.

"This New Hampshire courthouse witnessed the humble beginnings of a national figure."
Legal history & early career beginnings

Literary & Cultural Connections

1864

Nathaniel Hawthorne

Author of The Scarlet Letter

Hawthorne died while visiting Plymouth with former President Franklin Pierce. They were staying at the grand Pemigewasset House hotel. His connection to Plymouth ties our town to the literary currents of 19th century America, particularly the dark romanticism that contrasted with transcendental optimism.

"In this White Mountains hotel, one of America's great literary voices fell silent."
American literary history connection
1864

Franklin Pierce

14th U.S. President

The only U.S. President from New Hampshire, Pierce was traveling with his friend Nathaniel Hawthorne when the author died in Plymouth. Pierce's connection to our town highlights how even national figures found solace and companionship in the White Mountains region.

"A presidential companion in a moment of literary tragedy."
Presidential history & regional connections

Business & Sports Connections

Early 1900s

Draper & Maynard

Sporting Goods Innovators

This Plymouth-based company became the official supplier of baseball gloves to the Boston Red Sox. Their factory drew visits from baseball legends including Babe Ruth, connecting our small town to America's pastime in its golden age.

"From a Plymouth factory to Fenway Park—gloves that touched baseball history."
Local industry with national impact
1910s-1920s

Babe Ruth

Baseball Legend

The Sultan of Swat visited Plymouth to select custom gloves from the Draper & Maynard factory. His visits created legendary local stories and cemented Plymouth's surprising connection to Major League Baseball during its early glory days.

"Even baseball's greatest star found his way to our small New Hampshire town."
Sports history & celebrity connections

Voices of Plymouth Today

My family has lived in Plymouth for five generations. The independence our founders showed isn't just history—it's in how we approach challenges today, working together while respecting each person's ability to think for themselves.

Margaret Chen

Lifelong resident & local historian

Teaching at Plymouth State, I see how the transcendentalist values of self-reliance and connection to nature still resonate with our students. They come here not just for education, but to find something authentic in themselves.

Dr. Robert Thayer

Professor of Environmental Philosophy

As a carpenter working with local wood, I feel connected to those early settlers who built this town from the forest. There's a continuity in working with materials from this land—it keeps history alive in our hands.

James "Woody" Fitzgerald

Local craftsman & woodworker
